# The Manifesto for Agentic Software Engineering

*2025 — in the lineage of the Agile Manifesto (2001)*

---

We are uncovering better ways of building software by orchestrating AI agents, shipping continuously, and helping others do the same. Through this work we have come to value:

## Core Values

| We value this | | …over this |
|---|:---:|---|
| **Human orchestrators directing AI agents** | *over* | Siloed specialists and hand-offs |
| **Continuous, agent-driven delivery** | *over* | Planned sprints and release cycles |
| **Co-located, low-latency collaboration** | *over* | Async coordination and context switching |
| **Full-stack autonomous builders** | *over* | Separate PM, design, and engineering roles |

*That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more.*

---

## Nine Principles

1. **Delegate execution to agents.** Reserve human judgment for context, goals, trade-offs, and decisions agents cannot make. The mechanical work — boilerplate, refactors, scaffolding, test generation — belongs to the agent; taste, direction, and accountability remain with you.

2. **Small, co-located teams outpace large distributed orgs.** Two to ten builders in the same room minimise the loop between thought and shipped code. Headcount is not a substitute for bandwidth between minds.

3. **The ideal builder orchestrates agents, understands users, and ships without a hand-off.** Close the gap between product, design, and engineering wherever possible. The shortest path from an unmet user need to a deployed change is one person who can hold all three.

4. **Agentic coding shifts the bottleneck upstream.** When implementation is cheap, the constraints become specification, design, review, legal, and go-to-market. Reflect regularly on what is actually slowing you down — and attack that, not the code.

5. **Product thinking is the new scarce resource.** As agents absorb execution, knowing *what* to build outweighs the ability to type it. Every engineer should own some product judgement; every manager should be able to ship something themselves.

6. **Face-to-face is the lowest-latency protocol.** When speed is the constraint, default to co-location. Use async channels for durability and record-keeping, not for the decisions that matter most.

7. **Use agents to extend your reach — never to outsource your taste, ethics, or judgement.** Agents let one person operate across disciplines that once required a team. They do not replace the human responsibility for what is built, for whom, and at what cost.

8. **Speed compounds only on solid foundations.** Technical excellence, clear architecture, observable systems, and rigorous review still matter — and matter more, not less, when output accelerates. Velocity built on weak foundations is borrowed time.

9. **The constraint is no longer what you *can* build — it is what you *should* build.** Agentic tooling means almost anyone can ship almost anything. The questions that remain are human ones: is it worth doing, who is it for, and what does it change in the world? This is the golden age of the individual builder.
